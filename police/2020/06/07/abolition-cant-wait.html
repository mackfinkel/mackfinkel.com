Abolition Can't Wait: Data Issues in the #8CantWait Campaign

07 June 2020

Reforms with shaky evidence are no priority in the fight against police violence. Defunding and abolition are.

Earlier this week, the #8CantWait campaign launched, pointing activists towards 8 reforms that they could call on their local governments to implement. The call was met with lots of criticism. Should we redirect the energy of the moment to police reform rather than defunding? Does the data justify the conclusions of the report?

I took a quick look at the data and methodology on the study behind #8CantWait. There are issues.

  • Most acts of police violence do not result in death. The study only examined police killings, not including non-fatal shootings, beatings, taser use, tear gas, etc. The #8CantWait campaign uses “police violence” and “police killings” interchangeably. They are not the same.
  • This is a correlation study. It showed that departments with certain policies usually killed slightly fewer people. #8CantWait’s claims that these policies “reduce” or “decrease” police killings are not proved by this report.
  • The sample is small, the correlation is only moderate-weak, and the significance is not so strong either (n = 91, p = 0.024, r = -.24).
  • My hometown, Philadelphia, already has 7 of the 8 policies implemented. Yet Philly police have still been shamelessly brutal to protesters this past weekend.

Here is a graph showing the data from the report.

While there is a trend, there is no guarantee that a department with more of these policies is going to kill fewer people.

Rather than supporting reforms that may not reduce any killings and do not take away power from police departments, I support defunding and abolition. Abolitionists were quick to release a #8toAbolition campaign, which I support wholeheartedly.

Note: After criticism, it looks like #8CantWait removed the claim “Data proves that together these eight policies can decrease police violence by 72%” from their website. That’s good, because the claim isn’t true. It was still spread throughout the campaign launch.
